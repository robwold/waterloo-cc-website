The club's story

A long and proud history

Waterloo Chess Club has hosted serious chess in the Waterloo and Crosby area for over a century — through periods of championship success, a cast of distinguished members, and the occasional visit from chess royalty.

Since 1903

One of Merseyside's oldest clubs

Founded in 1903, Waterloo Chess Club is one of Merseyside's foremost chess clubs. For more than a hundred years it has hosted chess at various locations throughout the Waterloo and Crosby area, and has at times enjoyed periods of great success.

Competing under the governance of the Merseyside Chess Association, the club can boast of being a multiple-time winner of the Division 1 championship — most recently in 2003, its centennial year.

The club today

Two teams in the league

The first team will compete in the top division in the 2026-7 season, while the second team was promoted to the third division after winning the Division 4 title in 2026. We're also expecting to enter a third team due to the growth of the club and to give everyone who wants to play an opportunity.

Many of our players are also active in the competitive scene nationally — several members took part in the British Chess Championships when they were held in Liverpool in 2025.

Notable names

Players who've sat at our boards

Over the years the club has been home to several noteworthy players. Among them, International Master Gary Quillan; the author Thomas John Beach, who wrote on chess improvement alongside Col. Hugh O'Donel Alexander, famed for his work decoding the Enigma; and Dr Alan Ludgate, a former Irish Champion.

Ludgate was immortalised in Leonard Barden's book Play Better Chess, which features his historic defeat to the 11-year-old Nigel Short — a future Grandmaster.

1919

A world champion pays a visit

Waterloo has also played host to a variety of chess occasions, and the odd prestigious visitor. Most notable was World Champion José Raúl Capablanca, who gave a simultaneous exhibition at the club in 1919.

Remarkably, Waterloo's two top players — a father and son, both mathematicians — were each able to score a win against the Cuban great.

Given Capablanca's incredible record in simultaneous displays, this was a remarkable result.
